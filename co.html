About Us

As students on an international research trip to Colombia, our interdisciplinary fieldwork examines small-scale farming in the region, with a specific emphasis on Cacao cultivation. Our work includes engaging with local communities, visiting towns, and conducting in-depth discussions with residents to better understand their needs and aspirations. We will use Geographical Information Systems (GIS) to digitize and map data, creating visualizations of crop distribution and environmental patterns related to cocoa plants. By integrating field-collected data with GIS tools, our work provides valuable insights that serve as a foundational resource for decision-making. The collected data will be used by other teams working on this project to develop a deep-learning-based disease detection model and conduct a thorough biological analysis offering clear insights into farmers' crops and showcasing ways to optimize their crop growth. This opportunity fosters collaboration with Colombian farmers, promoting cross-cultural exchange while advancing farming techniques, utilizing GIS, and promoting sustainability.

Granja Luker

About Luker

Granja Luker is a leading research center focused on enhancing cocoa productivity, minimizing crop losses, and developing innovative agricultural practices to support cocoa farmers. For over 60 years, it has utilized specialized resources—nurseries, laboratories, experimental fields, and fermentation facilities—to improve cultivation techniques and address emerging challenges in cocoa production.

Luker's Challenges

Frosty Pod Rot is a fungal disease that can devastate cocoa production, causing 50–90% crop loss across Central America, South America, and West Africa. It spreads easily through water, wind, and pod movement, making containment difficult. The best prevention is early removal of infected pods, though detection at early stages remains a challenge.

Sustainability Efforts

Luker Farm is enhancing cocoa yields through its nursery program while testing biocontrol solutions for frosty pod disease. Researchers are evaluating Trichoderma (already in use) and Daldinia, which is undergoing safety testing to prevent unintended contamination. Integrating these efforts with advanced technology will improve crop resilience and sustainability.

Small Farmers

Local Cocoa Farms

During our trip, we visited a local farm in Belalcázar, where farmers are part of a cocoa farming community with over 120 members. They collaborate to develop new techniques and adopt innovative technologies to enhance harvest yields.

Regulating Productivity

Farmers use a university-provided weather station to monitor environmental factors affecting cocoa pods. They also operate fermentation facilities and processing machines for efficient cocoa handling.
